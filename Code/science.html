The Natural Cycles




Carbon Cycle


The carbon cycle describes the movement of carbon atoms from the atmosphere and back into the atmosphere. When organisms die, volcanic eruption happens, flames burn, oil and coal are burned, and a variety of other processes release carbon into the atmosphere. The four steps of Carbon cycle are Photosynthesis, Decomposition, Respiration and Combustion.




Water Cycle


The water cycle illustrates the continuous flow of water on Earth and even in the atmosphere. It's a complex process with many different processes. Fluid water evaporates into water vapour, condenses into clouds, and precipitates as rain and snow on the ground. The seven steps of water cycle are :evaporation, condensation, precipitation, interception, infiltration, percolation, transpiration, runoff, and storage.




Nitrogen Cycle


The nitrogen cycle is a biogeochemical cycle in which nitrogen is converted into multiple chemical forms as it passes through the atmospheric, land, and sea ecosystems. Fixation, ammonification, nitrification, and denitrification are all crucial components in the nitrogen cycle.




Phosphorus cycle


Phosphorus passes through stones, water, soil, sediments, and creatures in a loop. Rainfall and weathering cause phosphate ions and other ions to be released from stones throughout time. When a plant or animal dies, it decomposes, discharging organic phosphate into the soil..

Energy to make atp

Glucose provides the energy necessary to make ATP. Cellular respiration is a process through which cells convert glucose to ATP. The process of converting glucose into energy in the form of ATP is known as cellular respiration. Glucose must be processed into a form that the mitochondrion can use before cellular respiration can begin.

Human Circulatory System


Blood arteries that carry away the blood from and towards the heart make up the circulatory system. Blood travels away from the heart by arteries and back to the heart by veins. The circulatory system carries oxygen, nutrients, and hormones into cells all while eliminating products such as carbon dioxide.

Human Digestive System


Food is broken into nutrients like carbohydrates, fats, and protein by the digestive system. These can be absorbed into to the bloodstream and used for energy, growth, and healing by the body. Unwanted substances are tossed out as faeces.

Human Reproductive System


The male reproductive system is responsible for producing and depositing sperm, whereas the female reproductive system is responsible for producing egg cells as well as protecting and nourishing the foetus until birth. Gender differentiation is very great in humans.

Human Respiratory System


The respiratory system is a group of organs and tissues that assist in breathing. Your airways, lungs, and blood arteries are all part of it. The respiratory system includes the muscles that power your lungs. These components work together to transport oxygen throughout the body while also removing waste gases such as carbon dioxide.

Role of hormones in human growth


The roles of growth hormone include influencing our height, and helping build our bones and muscles. Natural levels of growth hormone fluctuate during the day, seemingly influenced by physical activity. For example, levels rise when we exercise. Growth hormone levels increase during childhood and peak during puberty.



Chemistry


Acids and Bases


An acid is a substance which has a PH level of lower than 7. It is also a hydrogen containing substance which can give a hydrogen ion. A base has a PH level below above 7 and it is capable of accepting a hydrogen ion. Acid tastes sour. Bases have a bitter taste. There are a few acids and bases in our own body. 1)The blood is slightly on the basic side. 2)The DNA is a complex nucleic acid.

PH scale


Now to identify whether something is an acid or base you don't need only an indicator but you also need a scale which can measure how much a substance is an acid or how much it is a base and in this case it is the PH scale which measure the PH level of a substance ultimately determined whether it is an acid or base.

PH Indicator


A PH indicator is something that indicates or states the ph level of an acid or base. For ex- litmus paper phenolphthalein. There are a few natural indicators on the list as well. For ex- Hibiscus, Turmeric, Onion and Blue pea flowers. There are few ways to identitify if an item is acid or bases without an indicator.

Stoichiometry

The computation of quantities in balanced chemical equation is called as stoichiometry. Stoichiometry tells us how many of each reactant we need to get enough of the desired result in a chemical process.Stoichiometry is important as You can't understand chemistry..

Covalent Bond

A covalent bond is a chemical connection that includes the exchange of two electrons between atoms. The two atomic nuclei capture those electrons at the very same time.

Diffusion

Diffusion is a process that results in a net flow of substance from a high-concentration zone to a low-concentration zone due to the unexpected mobility of molecules. The concentration gradient is discovered to be proportional to the diffusing substance's rate of flow.

Osmosis

Osmosis is defined as the spontaneous net movement or diffusion of solvent molecules through a selectively permeable membrane from a high water potential to a low water potential in a direction that tends to equalise the solute concentrations on both sides.

Weak Nuclear Force



The weak force works only across distances shorter than that of the atomic nucleus, but the electromagnetic force can extend over great distances (as shown in the light of stars extending across entire galaxies), weakening only as the distance square increases. It is one of the 4 fundamental forces in the universe.



why are metals good conductors of electricity and non metals are not?


The valence electrons in metal atoms are loosely bound by the nucleus and are movable. As a result, metals are excellent electrical conductors. The valence electrons of non-metal atoms are bound more strongly by the nucleus and are not movable. So there is no chance of electricity to pass through, therefore Metals are good conductors and non-metals are not.



Electrolyte

The breakdown of a substance into positively and negatively charged particles called ions, which migrate toward and are discharged at the negative and positive terminals (cathode and anode) of an electric circuit, respectively, transmits electric current. Strong electrolytes are chemical substance that conduct electricity when dissolved in water. A weak electrolyte is one that ionises in solution very little.


Conductors

Conductors are materials that enable electrons to freely flow through one particle to the next. Charge is applied through the free movement of electrons in conductors. Since metal's valence electron is not tightly bound by the nucleus and is moveable. So nearly all metals are good conductors.


Insulator

Quite the opposite of conductors, An insulator is a material that prevents energy from easily moving through it, such as electricity, heat, or cold. Plastic and wood are a few examples of insulator.


Ohm's Law

The flow through a conductor is proportional to the applied voltage from across conductor, as according Ohm's law. Since V is the voltage from across conductor & I is the current flowing through it, the formula is V=IR.Ohm's law is important for describing electric circuits since it links the voltage and current, with the resistance value moderating the relationship.
